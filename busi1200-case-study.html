BUSI 1200: Careers & Professional Development

Instructional Design Case Study

Course Context

BUSI 1200 is an 8-week, first-year course focused on career readiness, reflection, and professional skill articulation. The course serves students with varied levels of academic and professional preparedness, including many first-generation college students navigating institutional systems and career exploration for the first time.

Design Challenge & Constraints

The primary design challenge was creating a structured yet flexible learning experience within a compressed 8-week timeline. Students entered the course with wide-ranging familiarity with professional development concepts, making clarity of expectations and cognitive load management essential. The course needed to balance skill development with reflection while maintaining accessibility within a Canvas-based environment.

Instructional Design Approach

Course design centered on modular, Canvas-native pages that emphasized consistency, predictability, and guided progression. Learning experiences were scaffolded to reduce overwhelm and support student confidence through repeated cycles of practice, reflection, and feedback.

  • Weekly modular structure with clear completion pathways
  • Prerequisite gating to encourage sequential engagement
  • Practice-based activities over passive content consumption
  • Reflection used as both a learning and assessment tool

Design in Practice: Selected Examples

The following examples highlight how instructional design principles were translated into concrete learning experiences within BUSI 1200. Each example reflects intentional design choices made to support clarity, engagement, and skill articulation.

Design Example: Career Research Module

Design goal: Support students in exploring potential career pathways while reducing intimidation associated with labor market data and unfamiliar research tools.

Instructional move: The module guided students through career research using structured prompts and curated resources, allowing them to focus on interpretation rather than navigation. Scaffolded questions helped students translate research into personal insights.

Why it works: By breaking research into manageable steps and emphasizing reflection, students were able to engage meaningfully with career information without becoming overwhelmed.

Design Example: Resume Court

Design goal: Encourage students to critically evaluate resumes and articulate professional strengths without the pressure of immediate self-presentation.

Instructional move: Students analyzed sample resumes through guided criteria, shifting focus from personal performance to skill recognition and constructive critique.

Why it works: This approach lowered affective barriers and helped students develop evaluative skills that later supported their own resume development.

Use of AI in Instructional Design

AI tools were used as a support for instructional design by assisting with the drafting of instructional language, generating example scenarios, and refining clarity. All AI-assisted content was reviewed and aligned with learning objectives, ensuring that pedagogy—not automation—guided design decisions.

Reflection & Iteration

Throughout the course, design decisions were refined in response to student engagement patterns and feedback. Observations of student confusion or overload informed adjustments to instructional language and pacing. Future iterations will continue to refine the balance between structure and flexibility as student needs evolve.
