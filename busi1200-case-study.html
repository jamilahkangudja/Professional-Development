BUSI 1200: Careers & Professional Development

Instructional Design Case Study

Course Context

BUSI 1200 is an 8-week, first-year course focused on career readiness, reflection, and professional skill articulation. The course supports students with varied levels of academic and professional preparedness, including many first-generation college students navigating institutional systems and career exploration for the first time.

Design Challenge & Constraints

The primary design challenge was creating a structured yet flexible learning experience within a compressed 8-week timeline. Students entered the course with uneven familiarity with professional development concepts, requiring intentional scaffolding to manage cognitive load while maintaining momentum.

Instructional Design Approach

Course design centered on modular, Canvas-native pages that emphasized consistency, predictability, and guided progression. Learning experiences were intentionally sequenced to reinforce connections between goal setting, self-assessment, professional documents, and career exploration.

  • Weekly modular structure with clear completion pathways
  • Prerequisite gating to encourage sequential engagement
  • Practice-based activities over passive content consumption
  • Reflection used as both a learning and assessment tool

Design in Practice: Selected Instructional Artifacts

The following instructional artifacts demonstrate how course design principles were translated into student-facing learning experiences. Each artifact reflects intentional decisions to support clarity, engagement, and skill articulation across the progression of the course.

ONET Career Explorer Guide

Design goal: Prepare students to navigate labor market data and career exploration tools without becoming overwhelmed by unfamiliar terminology or interface complexity.

Instructional move: A guided ONET Career Explorer Guide was developed to walk students through key ONET concepts and navigation prior to engaging directly with the ONET Online platform. The activity was intentionally placed after goal setting, self-assessment, and professional document development to encourage reflection on alignment.

Why it works: By front-loading conceptual understanding and sequencing the activity after foundational self-work, students were able to interpret career data through the lens of their existing goals and materials.

Resume Forensic Lab: Applicant Tracking System (ATS) Simulation

Design goal: Help students understand how applicant tracking systems evaluate resumes and why formatting and structure directly impact screening outcomes.

Instructional move: An interactive Resume Forensic Lab was designed to simulate how ATS parses resume content. Students observed how improper formatting, images, and unconventional headings result in resumes being deprioritized or rejected.

Why it works: Making the ATS process visible transformed an abstract concept into a concrete learning experience, leading to more intentional formatting decisions in subsequent resume drafts.

Use of AI in Instructional Design

AI tools were used to support instructional design by assisting with the drafting of instructional language, generating example scenarios, and refining clarity. All AI-assisted content was reviewed and aligned with learning objectives to ensure that pedagogy, not automation, guided design decisions.

Reflection & Iteration

Course design decisions were refined throughout the term based on student engagement patterns and feedback. Observations of confusion or overload informed adjustments to instructional language and pacing. Future iterations will continue to strengthen alignment between structure, reflection, and professional skill development.
